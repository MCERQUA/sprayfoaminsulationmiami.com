Miami runs air conditioning harder and longer than almost anywhere in the country. That makes energy bills the number-one reason homeowners call us about spray foam — and the number-one reason they end up happy they did it. Here's what actually happens to your bills, why it happens, and what to realistically expect.

## The headline number: 30–50%

For the great majority of Miami homes, properly installed spray foam reduces **cooling costs by 30–50%**. Some homes see more, some less, depending on the starting condition of the insulation and the scope of the project. Across the 1,000+ South Florida properties we've insulated since 2008, that 30–50% range is the consistent, real-world result.

On a home with summer electric bills of $300–$450, that's roughly **$100–$200 in monthly savings** during peak cooling months — and meaningful savings the rest of the year too, because even Miami winters require some conditioning and dehumidification.

## Why spray foam works so well in South Florida

To understand the savings, you have to understand what your AC is actually fighting.

Traditional insulation — fiberglass batts, blown-in cellulose — works by slowing conductive heat flow. But in Miami, the bigger enemy is **convective heat gain**: hot, humid outside air pushing into your attic and walls through the tiny gaps, cracks, and penetrations that every home has. Fiberglass and cellulose don't seal those gaps. They filter air; they don't stop it.

Spray foam is different. It **expands in place to fill every gap and crack**, creating a continuous air barrier. That air barrier is what blocks the convective heat gain driving your cooling load — and it's why foam dramatically outperforms fiberglass in real Miami conditions, even when the fiberglass has a higher label R-value.

There's a second factor: **humidity control**. Your AC spends a huge amount of energy dehumidifying the air that leaks into your home. By sealing the envelope, spray foam cuts that dehumidification load too. The result is a home that's not just cooler but drier and more comfortable at a higher thermostat setting.

## What that means for your AC

Homeowners consistently report two changes after spray foam:

1. **The AC cycles normally.** Instead of running constantly from morning to night, it kicks on, brings the home to temperature, and shuts off — the way it was designed to.
2. **You can raise the thermostat.** Because the home holds temperature and humidity better, many homeowners find they're comfortable at 76–78°F instead of 72°F. Every degree you raise the thermostat saves roughly 6–8% on cooling.

Both of these compound. Less run-time, higher comfortable setpoint, lower bills.

## Payback: how fast it pays for itself

Take a realistic Miami scenario:

- Summer bills averaging **$350/month**.
- Conservative **35% reduction** from spray foam.
- Savings of roughly **$1,470/year**.
- A $4,500 attic project pays back in about **3 years**.

After payback, the savings continue for the life of the foam — and spray foam lasts **decades without degrading**, unlike fiberglass which settles and loses effectiveness. A spray foam investment made today will still be saving you money in 20 or 30 years.

## When the savings are biggest

The largest savings come on homes that are the worst starting point:

- **Older homes** with settled or failed fiberglass and cellulose.
- **Homes with hot second floors** or rooms that never cool evenly.
- **Homes with oversized AC** that short-cycles and never properly dehumidifies.
- **Homes in direct sun with poor existing attic insulation.**

If your home fits any of these, you're likely to see savings at the higher end of the range.
