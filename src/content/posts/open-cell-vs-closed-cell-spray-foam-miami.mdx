Open-cell or closed-cell? It's the single most common question we get from Miami homeowners — and the most important one to answer correctly. The wrong choice in the wrong place can mean moisture problems, underperformance, or money wasted. Here's an honest, no-upsell breakdown for South Florida properties.

## The core difference

Both are spray polyurethane foam, but they're manufactured with different cell structures that give them very different properties:

- **Open-cell foam** is made of cells that are intentionally broken open. It's soft, spongy, lower-density, and expands aggressively to fill cavities. Air and vapor can pass through it.
- **Closed-cell foam** is made of closed, packed cells. It's dense, rigid, higher-density, and acts as an air barrier, vapor barrier, and water resistor all at once.

That structural difference drives everything that follows.

## Side-by-side: the real numbers

| Property | Open-Cell | Closed-Cell |
|---|---|---|
| **R-value per inch** | ~R-3.7 | ~R-6.5 |
| **Vapor behavior** | Permeable — lets assemblies dry | Class II vapor retarder — blocks vapor |
| **Moisture barrier** | No | Yes |
| **Rigidity** | Soft, flexible | Dense, rigid, adds structural strength |
| **Sound control** | Excellent | Good |
| **Cost** | Lower ($$) | Higher ($$$) |
| **Typical thickness** | 5.5–7+ inches | 2–3 inches |

## Where open-cell belongs

Open-cell is the right product when you want strong air-sealing and sound control at a lower cost, and where the assembly is designed to manage moisture by drying inward:

- **Interior walls and ceilings** for soundproofing between rooms, condos, and units.
- **Attics brought into the conditioned envelope**, when humidity is actively managed and the assembly can dry.
- **Condo demising walls** and floor separations where neighbor noise is the primary complaint.
- **Budget-conscious whole-home packages** where vapor control is handled elsewhere.

Open-cell's softness is a feature for sound — its porous structure absorbs airborne noise better than almost any other insulation at its price point.

## Where closed-cell belongs

Closed-cell is the top-tier choice for any assembly that faces moisture, structural demand, or tight space constraints — which in South Florida is a lot of them:

- **Roof decks and unvented attics** where you want a true vapor barrier and structural reinforcement.
- **Crawl spaces and raised floors** exposed to ground moisture.
- **Flood-zone and hurricane-exposed properties** — closed-cell resists storm-driven water and stiffens the building.
- **Metal buildings, warehouses, and cold storage** needing high R-value in limited depth.
- **Exterior wall assemblies** in CBS, frame, and metal construction.

For Miami Beach, the Keys, and anywhere in a flood zone, closed-cell is almost always the right recommendation.

## Can you mix them?

Yes — and for many Miami homes that's the ideal approach. A common, cost-effective package is **closed-cell in the crawl space and on moisture-prone exterior walls**, paired with **open-cell in interior partitions for sound** and in attic areas where the assembly is designed to dry. This puts the higher-performance product exactly where moisture defense matters and uses the lower-cost product where sound and air-sealing are the goals.
